    B) Open-ended, student-led inquiry and dialogue
    C) Rote memorization of facts
    D) Individual work only
    Answer: B) Open-ended, student-led inquiry and dialogue
    Explanation: In a Socratic seminar, students engage in a formal discussion based on a text or topic, with the teacher acting as a facilitator rather than a lecturer. 
     

    The "peer tutoring" strategy involves:
    A) Students teaching other students
    B) The teacher providing one-on-one instruction
    C) Students learning from a computer program
    D) Parents teaching their children at home
    Answer: A) Students teaching other students
    Explanation: Peer tutoring is an instructional strategy where students work in pairs or small groups, with one student taking on the role of the tutor to help the other learn. 
     

    The "closure" part of a lesson plan is designed to:
    A) Introduce new material
    B) Summarize the lesson and check for final understanding
    C) Assign homework
    D) Manage transitions
    Answer: B) Summarize the lesson and check for final understanding
    Explanation: Closure is the conclusion of the lesson, where the teacher helps students synthesize what they have learned and make connections to future learning. 
     

    The "debate" teaching strategy is particularly effective for developing:
    A) Memorization skills
    B) Research, public speaking, and critical thinking skills
    C) Artistic skills
    D) Mathematical skills
    Answer: B) Research, public speaking, and critical thinking skills
    Explanation: Debates require students to research a topic, formulate arguments, and think critically about opposing viewpoints, developing a range of valuable skills. 
     

    A teacher who uses "proximity" by moving closer to a disruptive student is using a:
    A) Punitive technique
    B) Non-verbal classroom management strategy
    C) Formative assessment strategy
    D) Motivational speech
    Answer: B) Non-verbal classroom management strategy
    Explanation: Proximity control is a subtle, non-verbal technique where the teacher's physical presence near a student can help refocus their behavior without disrupting the class. 
     

    The "whole language" approach to teaching reading emphasizes:
    A) Phonics and decoding skills in isolation
    B) Learning to read through exposure to complete, meaningful texts
    C) Memorizing sight words only
    D) Focusing only on grammar rules
    Answer: B) Learning to read through exposure to complete, meaningful texts
    Explanation: The whole language approach views reading as a natural, holistic process where children learn to read by being immersed in literature and meaningful print. 
     
 

    The main purpose of "diagnostic assessment" is to:
    A) Assign a final grade at the end of the year
    B) Identify students' strengths and weaknesses before instruction begins
    C) Monitor progress during a unit
    D) Compare a school's performance to others
    Answer: B) Identify students' strengths and weaknesses before instruction begins
    Explanation: Diagnostic assessment is a pre-instructional tool used to determine students' prior knowledge and skill levels, allowing the teacher to plan appropriate instruction. 
     

    An assessment that measures what it is intended to measure is said to be:
    A) Reliable
    B) Valid
    C) Objective
    D) Standardized
    Answer: B) Valid
    Explanation: Validity refers to the accuracy of a test in measuring what it claims to measure. A valid test accurately assesses the intended construct. 
     

    An assessment that yields consistent results over time is said to be:
    A) Valid
    B) Reliable
    C) Norm-referenced
    D) Criterion-referenced
    Answer: B) Reliable
    Explanation: Reliability refers to the consistency of a measure. A reliable test will produce similar results under consistent conditions. 
     

    A test that compares a student's performance to that of a larger group is called a:
    A) Criterion-referenced test
    B) Norm-referenced test
    C) Summative test
    D) Formative test
    Answer: B) Norm-referenced test
    Explanation: Norm-referenced tests (e.g., IQ tests, standardized achievement tests) score an individual in comparison to the performance of a norm group. 
     

    A test that measures a student's performance against a fixed standard or criterion is called a:
    A) Norm-referenced test
    B) Criterion-referenced test
    C) Aptitude test
    D) Diagnostic test
    Answer: B) Criterion-referenced test
    Explanation: Criterion-referenced tests (e.g., a driving test, a final exam with a passing score of 70%) measure whether a student has mastered a specific body of knowledge. 
     

    "Authentic assessment" is best characterized by:
    A) Multiple-choice questions
    B) Tasks that resemble real-world challenges
    C) Timed, written exams
    D) Fill-in-the-blank questions
    Answer: B) Tasks that resemble real-world challenges
    Explanation: Authentic assessment evaluates students' ability to apply what they have learned to meaningful, real-world tasks, such as projects, portfolios, or performances. 
     

    The process of judging the effectiveness of a program, curriculum, or policy is called:
    A) Measurement
    B) Assessment
    C) Evaluation
    D) Testing
    Answer: C) Evaluation
    Explanation: Evaluation is a broader process that involves making judgments about the value or effectiveness of something based on collected data (assessment). 
     

    Which of the following is an example of a "formal assessment"?
    A) Observing a student during group work
    B) Asking a question during a lesson
    C) A unit test
    D) A class discussion
    Answer: C) A unit test
    Explanation: Formal assessments are structured, systematic methods of measuring student learning, such as tests, quizzes, and essays. Informal assessments are more spontaneous, like observations. 
     

    Which of the following is an example of an "informal assessment"?
    A) A standardized test
    B) A final exam
    C) Teacher's observation notes
    D) A research paper
    Answer: C) Teacher's observation notes
    Explanation: Informal assessments are not data-driven but rather content and performance-driven, such as observations, checklists, and class participation. 
     

    The "mean" of a set of scores is:
    A) The middle value when the scores are arranged in order
    B) The value that appears most frequently
    C) The arithmetic average
    D) The difference between the highest and lowest scores
    Answer: C) The arithmetic average
    Explanation: The mean is calculated by summing all the scores and dividing by the number of scores. 
     

    The "median" of a set of scores is:
    A) The arithmetic average
    B) The value that appears most frequently
    C) The middle value when the scores are arranged in order
    D) The most frequent score
    Answer: C) The middle value when the scores are arranged in order
    Explanation: The median is the middle point of a data set, which is useful when the data includes outliers that might skew the mean. 
     

    The "mode" of a set of scores is:
    A) The arithmetic average
    B) The middle value
    C) The value that appears most frequently
    D) The range of scores
    Answer: C) The value that appears most frequently
    Explanation: The mode is the most frequently occurring value in a data set. 
     

    The "range" of a set of scores is:
    A) The average score
    B) The difference between the highest and lowest scores
    C) The middle score
    D) The most frequent score
    Answer: B) The difference between the highest and lowest scores
    Explanation: The range is a simple measure of dispersion, calculated by subtracting the lowest score from the highest score. 
     

    "Standard deviation" is a measure of:
    A) Central tendency
    B) The spread or variability of scores
    C) The validity of a test
    D) The reliability of a test
    Answer: B) The spread or variability of scores
    Explanation: Standard deviation measures how spread out the scores are from the mean. A small standard deviation indicates that scores are close to the mean, while a large one indicates they are widely spread. 
     

    An "aptitude test" is designed to measure:
    A) What a student has already learned
    B) A student's potential to learn a new skill
    C) A student's personality traits
    D) A student's interests
    Answer: B) A student's potential to learn a new skill
    Explanation: Aptitude tests are used to predict a person's ability to learn a new skill or succeed in a particular area. 
     

    An "achievement test" is designed to measure:
    A) A student's potential for future success
    B) What a student has learned or accomplished
    C) A student's intelligence
    D) A student's interests
    Answer: B) What a student has learned or accomplished
    Explanation: Achievement tests assess what a student has learned from specific instruction, such as a chapter test or a final exam. 
     

    The "item analysis" of a test helps to determine:
    A) The total score of a student
    B) The difficulty and discriminating power of each question
    C) The reliability of the entire test
    D) The validity of the test
    Answer: B) The difficulty and discriminating power of each question
    Explanation: Item analysis examines the performance of individual test items to see how difficult they were and how well they differentiated between high- and low-scoring students. 
     

    A question that nearly all students answer correctly has a high:
    A) Discrimination index
    B) Difficulty index
    C) Distractor efficiency
    D) Validity coefficient
    Answer: B) Difficulty index
    Explanation: The difficulty index is the proportion of students who answered the item correctly. A high index means the item was easy. 
     

    A question that high-scoring students answer correctly and low-scoring students answer incorrectly has a high:
    A) Difficulty index
    B) Discrimination index
    C) Distractor efficiency
    D) Reliability coefficient
    Answer: B) Discrimination index
    Explanation: The discrimination index measures how well an item differentiates between students who have mastered the material and those who have not. 
     

    "Grading on a curve" is an example of:
    A) Criterion-referenced grading
    B) Norm-referenced grading
    C) Authentic assessment
    D) Formative assessment
    Answer: B) Norm-referenced grading
    Explanation: Grading on a curve assigns grades based on a student's performance relative to the performance of the rest of the group, which is the essence of norm-referencing. 
     

    The "ceiling effect" in a test occurs when:
    A) The test is too difficult for most students
    B) The test is too easy for high-achieving students
    C) The test has a time limit
    D) The test is not valid
    Answer: B) The test is too easy for high-achieving students
    Explanation: The ceiling effect occurs when a test cannot measure the true ability of high-performing students because the items are not challenging enough. 
     

    The "floor effect" in a test occurs when:
    A) The test is too easy for most students
    B) The test is too difficult for low-achieving students
    C) The test has no time limit
    D) The test is not reliable
    Answer: B) The test is too difficult for low-achieving students
    Explanation: The floor effect occurs when a test is too difficult, and even the lowest-performing students score at or near the bottom, preventing an accurate measurement of their ability. 
     

    A "rubric" is a scoring guide used to evaluate:
    A) Only multiple-choice tests
    B) Performance-based assessments like essays or projects
    C) Only standardized tests
    D) Only final exams
    Answer: B) Performance-based assessments like essays or projects
    Explanation: A rubric lists the criteria for a task and describes different levels of performance for each criterion, providing a clear and objective way to score complex, subjective tasks. 
     

    The "right to education" act mandates that evaluation should be:
    A) Based only on written exams
    B) Comprehensive and continuous (CCE)
    C) Conducted only at the end of the year
    D) Used for ranking students
    Answer: B) Comprehensive and continuous (CCE)
    Explanation: The RTE Act 2009 emphasized the implementation of Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE) to reduce stress and assess students holistically. 
     

    The primary goal of "self-assessment" is to:
    A) Assign a grade
    B) Help students develop metacognitive skills and take ownership of their learning
    C) Compare students to each other
    D) Fulfill a requirement for the teacher
    Answer: B) Help students develop metacognitive skills and take ownership of their learning
    Explanation: Self-assessment encourages students to reflect on their own work, identify strengths and weaknesses, and set goals for improvement, fostering metacognition and independence. 
     

    "Peer assessment" involves:
    A) The teacher assessing the student
    B) The student assessing their own work
    C) Students assessing the work of their peers
    D) The principal assessing the teacher
    Answer: C) Students assessing the work of their peers
    Explanation: Peer assessment is a process where students evaluate the work of their classmates according to specific criteria, which can deepen their own understanding. 
     

    The term "washback effect" refers to the influence of:
    A) Teaching on assessment
    B) Assessment on teaching and learning
    C) Parents on student learning
    D) Curriculum on textbooks
    Answer: B) Assessment on teaching and learning
    Explanation: The washback effect (or backwash) is the impact that testing has on teaching methods and student learning behaviors. High-stakes tests, for example, can strongly influence what is taught. 
     

    A test that measures a student's ability to think critically and solve novel problems is assessing:
    A) Rote knowledge
    B) Higher-order thinking skills
    C) Basic factual recall
    D) Simple comprehension
    Answer: B) Higher-order thinking skills
    Explanation: Higher-order thinking skills involve the upper levels of Bloom's Taxonomy, such as analyzing, evaluating, and creating. 
     

    The "percentile rank" of a score indicates:
    A) The percentage of questions answered correctly
    B) The percentage of people in the norm group who scored lower
    C) The grade a student received
    D) The average score of the group
    Answer: B) The percentage of people in the norm group who scored lower
    Explanation: A percentile rank shows how a score compares to other scores. A score at the 80th percentile means the student scored higher than 80% of the norm group. 
     

    The use of "anecdotal records" is a form of:
    A) Summative assessment
    B) Formal assessment
    C) Informal, observational assessment
    D) Standardized testing
    Answer: C) Informal, observational assessment
    Explanation: An anecdotal record is a short, objective, descriptive account of a significant event or behavior observed by the teacher. 
     

    A "checklist" in assessment is used to:
    A) Provide detailed feedback on the quality of work
    B) Record the presence or absence of specific traits or behaviors
    C) Assign a numerical score to a performance
    D) Measure student attitudes
    Answer: B) Record the presence or absence of specific traits or behaviors
    Explanation: A checklist is a simple tool for observing and marking whether specific behaviors, skills, or criteria are present or absent. 
     

    A "rating scale" is used to assess:
    A) The presence or absence of a trait
    B) The degree or quality of a trait along a continuum
    C) Only factual knowledge
    D) Only final products
    Answer: B) The degree or quality of a trait along a continuum
    Explanation: A rating scale (e.g., 1-5, or 'poor to excellent') is used to judge the quality or degree of a behavior, skill, or performance, unlike a checklist which is dichotomous. 
     

    The process of translating a test into different languages while maintaining its validity is called:
    A) Standardization
    B) Item analysis
    C) Test equating
    D) Test adaptation
    Answer: D) Test adaptation
    Explanation: Test adaptation is a rigorous process that goes beyond simple translation to ensure that a test is culturally and linguistically appropriate and valid for different groups. 
     

    The "halo effect" in grading can lead to:
    A) Objective assessment
    B) A teacher's overall impression of a student influencing their judgment of specific work
    C) Accurate measurement of student ability
    D) Increased test validity
    Answer: B) A teacher's overall impression of a student influencing their judgment of specific work
    Explanation: The halo effect is a bias where a teacher's positive (or negative) general feeling about a student affects their evaluation of the student's specific work. 
     

    Which of the following best describes "Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation" (CCE)?
    A) A single final exam
    A) A single final exam
    B) A system of assessment that covers all aspects of a student's development and is continuous throughout the year
    C) A system that only assesses academic subjects
    D) A system that relies only on pen-and-paper tests
    Answer: B) A system of assessment that covers all aspects of a student's development and is continuous throughout the year
    Explanation: CCE aimed to assess students holistically (cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains) through a variety of tools conducted continuously during the teaching-learning process. 
     

    "Value-added assessment" measures:
    A) A student's absolute score on a test
    B) The academic growth a student makes over a period of time
    C) The student's performance compared to a national average
    D) The student's performance on a single project
    Answer: B) The academic growth a student makes over a period of time
    Explanation: Value-added models attempt to measure the impact of a teacher or school on student growth by tracking student performance over time. 
     

    The "split-half reliability" method involves:
    A) Administering the same test twice
    B) Comparing two different versions of a test
    C) Dividing a test into two halves and correlating the scores
    D) Having different graders score the same test
    Answer: C) Dividing a test into two halves and correlating the scores
    Explanation: Split-half reliability is a measure of internal consistency. A test is split in half (e.g., odd/even questions), and the scores on the two halves are correlated. 
     

    "Test-retest reliability" involves:
    A) Giving the same test to the same group on two different occasions
    B) Splitting the test into two halves
    C) Using two different forms of a test
    D) Checking the consistency of graders
    Answer: A) Giving the same test to the same group on two different occasions
    Explanation: Test-retest reliability measures the consistency of results over time by administering the same test to the same individuals at two different points in time. 
     

    The purpose of "distractors" in a multiple-choice question is to:
    A) Make the question easier
    B) Provide plausible but incorrect options to test student understanding
    C) Add extra information to the question
    D) Confuse all students
    Answer: B) Provide plausible but incorrect options to test student understanding
    Explanation: Good distractors are plausible answers that appeal to students with common misconceptions, thereby effectively discriminating between those who know the material and those who do not. 
     

    The process of setting a "cut score" on a test is used to:
    A) Calculate the mean score
    B) Determine the passing/failing point
    C) Measure the reliability of the test
    D) Analyze the difficulty of items
    Answer: B) Determine the passing/failing point
    Explanation: A cut score is a predetermined point on a score scale that is used to classify test takers into categories, such as pass/fail or proficient/below proficient.
